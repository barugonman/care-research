## 2. Legal & Financial Framework — What the Plan Has to Solve

### A. Massachusetts probate (Essex County) — what avoidance saves

Filings go to **Essex Probate and Family Court** (Salem) under the **Massachusetts Uniform Probate Code (M.G.L. c. 190B)**. Direct court costs are modest (~$390 filing fee). The real drag is indirect: **attorney fees ($2,500–$15,000+), executor compensation (1–3%), publication, appraisals, and the one-year creditor-claim window** under § 3-803. Practitioners commonly cite **3–5% of gross estate** total drag, so a $1.0–1.3M estate sees **$30K–$60K** of avoidable cost. Timeline runs **9–18 months** from death to close-out. And probate filings — including the will and the asset inventory — become public record.

Source: [Probate and Family Court filing fees, Mass.gov](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/probate-and-family-court-filing-fees) · [Spinnaker Probate — MA probate cost](https://spinnakerprobate.com/probate-cost-ma/) · [Legacy Care Law — MA probate timeline](https://legacycarelaw.com/blog/probate-2/understanding-the-massachusetts-probate-process-and-timeline/).

### B. Revocable living trust — the right primary tool

The **Massachusetts Uniform Trust Code, M.G.L. c. 203E** (in effect since July 8, 2012) presumes a trust is revocable unless the document says otherwise (§ 602). The father, as settlor, signs a **Declaration / Agreement of Trust**, names himself as initial trustee and his children as successor trustees, and **funds** the trust by deeding Preston Beach Rd to himself "as trustee of the John Allard Revocable Trust dated [date]."

> **The single most common drafting failure in MA is creating a trust and never funding it.** A signed-but-unfunded trust is just expensive paper; the home remains in the probate estate. Funding = recording the deed; retitling brokerage/bank accounts; coordinating beneficiary designations.

Source: [M.G.L. c. 203E](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/Partii/Titleii/Chapter203e) · [§ 602 — Revocability](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartII/TitleII/Chapter203e/Article6/Section602).

### C. Why **revocable** beats **irrevocable** as the primary vehicle

| Tool | Probate avoided? | Step-up preserved? | MassHealth countable? | Estate-recovery exposure? | Father retains control? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Will only (current state) | **No** | Yes | Yes | **Yes — probate estate is recoverable** | Yes |
| **Revocable trust (recommended)** | **Yes** | **Yes (full step-up)** | Yes (revocable = countable) | **No (non-probate)** | Yes |
| MAPT (irrevocable, retained SPOA) — only after 5-yr clock | Yes | Yes (retained § 2038 power) | No (after lookback) | No | **No** — gives up principal |
| Outright gift / quitclaim to kids | Yes | **No — carryover basis** | **5-yr transfer penalty** | No | **No** |
| Joint tenancy with children added | Yes | Only ½ step-up | Triggers transfer issues | Mixed | Partial |

The revocable trust is the only structure that *simultaneously* avoids probate, preserves a full step-up under IRC § 1014, leaves John in unilateral control, and is cleanly compatible with HUD's HECM rules. **Massachusetts does NOT recognize transfer-on-death (TOD) deeds** — those are unavailable. **Lady Bird / enhanced life-estate deeds** are not statutorily authorized; a related "life estate with reserved special power of appointment" structure has limited MA appellate support (*Skye v. Hession*, 71 Mass. App. Ct. 819 (2008)) but is a niche tool, not a primary recommendation.

Source: [DeedClaim — Massachusetts deeds](https://www.deedclaim.com/massachusetts/) · [Nolo — Avoiding Probate in MA](https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/massachusetts-avoiding-probate-31681.html) · [Elderlaw.info on *Skye v. Hession*](https://elderlaw.info/2017/05/01/reservation-and-exercise-of-special-power-of-appointment-in-deed-is-approved-by-massachusetts-appeals-court-in-2007-case-of-skye-v-hession/).

### D. The reverse mortgage — the single biggest technical risk

Federal HUD regulations at **24 C.F.R. Part 206** and **HUD Mortgagee Letter 93-22** permit transferring a HECM-burdened property into an **eligible inter vivos revocable trust** without triggering the loan's due-and-payable clause, *provided* the trust meets specific requirements:

1. The trust is revocable by the borrower at any time.
2. At least one original HECM borrower (John) is a beneficiary during life.
3. Other current beneficiaries are eligible (adult, identifiable individuals).
4. The trust does not extinguish the borrower's right to occupy as principal residence.
5. The servicer reviews and approves the trust documents in writing.

**Process:** draft the trust, send to the loan servicer for review, obtain written consent, **then** record the deed. Allow 30–60 days for servicer review. An unconsented transfer can be flagged by the servicer as a default trigger even if it would have qualified — they need the documentation in their underwriting file.

**On John's death**, the HECM matures and the children must satisfy the loan within **6 months** (HUD-approved 90-day extensions, up to 12 months total). Their options:

1. **Pay off the balance in full** (~$280K) and keep the home.
2. **Refinance into a conventional mortgage** in their own name(s).
3. **Sell** and pay off, keeping the equity (~$1.0M+ pre-cost).
4. **"Rule of 95"** if balance > FMV (not applicable here).
5. **Deed-in-lieu of foreclosure** — surrender title without deficiency.

With $1.3M FMV and $280K balance, **all four real options are open.** The family should pre-decide whether the goal is "keep the house" (refinance pre-decided) or "liquidate equity" (sell). Either decision is dramatically cleaner executed by a successor trustee than by a court-appointed personal representative.

> **Worth flagging — servicer-specific quirks.** Different HECM servicers (Reverse Mortgage Funding, Finance of America Reverse, Mutual of Omaha, Champion, etc.) layer their own documentary requirements on top of HUD's baseline. Pull the exact current requirements from the servicer named on the most recent statement.

Source: [eCFR — 24 CFR Part 206](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-B/chapter-II/subchapter-B/part-206) · [HUD ML 93-22](https://www.hud.gov/sites/documents/93-22ml.txt) · [NRMLA — Trusts and HECMs (May 2025)](https://www.nrmlaonline.org/2025/05/29/key-takeaways-on-trusts-and-hecms) · [NRMLA — What Do I Do When My Loan Is Due (PDF)](https://www.nrmlaonline.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/What-Do-I-Do-When-My-Loan-is-Due-UPDATED-July-2021.pdf) · [Mass.gov — MA Law About Reverse Mortgages](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-law-about-reverse-mortgages-home-equity-conversion-mortgages-hecm).

### E. Step-up in basis — why this drives the irrevocable-vs-revocable decision

Under **IRC § 1014**, property acquired from a decedent receives a basis equal to fair market value on the date of death. The math:

- Original basis: **$65,000** (1976) plus capital improvements (request records).
- Current FMV: **~$1,300,000**.
- **Embedded gain: ~$1,235,000.**
- Combined federal LTCG (20%) + NIIT (3.8%) + MA income tax (5%) ≈ **23.8–28.8%** on the gain.
- **Avoidable tax with full step-up: ~$294K–$356K.**

Step-up applies *if and only if* the asset is includible in the decedent's gross estate. Includibility outcomes:

- **Revocable trust:** Includible (IRC §§ 2036/2038). **Full step-up.** ✅
- **Outright lifetime gift:** Donee takes carryover basis (IRC § 1015). **No step-up.** ❌
- **Standard irrevocable trust** (grantor relinquishes powers): Not includible. **No step-up** (Rev. Rul. 2023-2). ❌
- **MAPT with retained testamentary special/limited power of appointment:** Power causes inclusion under § 2038, so step-up *is* preserved while the gift was complete enough for the MassHealth look-back to start running. ✅ — but only if the trust is drafted carefully.

**Implication:** Even if the family wants to start the MassHealth clock with a MAPT, the MAPT must contain a **retained limited power of appointment** to preserve step-up. Generic MAPT templates frequently omit this. **Ask candidate attorneys directly about their default MAPT drafting.**

Source: [IRC § 1014 (Tax Notes)](https://www.taxnotes.com/research/federal/usc26/1014) · [Grant Thornton — IRS no step-up for irrevocable grantor trust property](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/newsletters/tax/2023/hot-topics/apr-25/irs-rules-no-basis-step-up-at-death-for-grantor-trust-property) · [WealthCounsel — MAPT and LPOA](https://info.wealthcounsel.com/blog/the-medicaid-asset-protection-trust-and-the-limited-power-of-appointment) · [Burner Law — Medicaid Trusts and Step-Up Basis](https://burnerlaw.com/blog/medicaid-trusts-and-step-up-basis/).

### F. Massachusetts estate tax — likely a non-issue here, but verify

**Chapter 50 of the Acts of 2023** (signed Oct 4, 2023; retroactive to Jan 1, 2023) raised the MA estate-tax effective exemption from $1M to **$2M** and eliminated the cliff — tax now applies only to the excess over $2M.

For John's estate:
- Home equity: $1.3M − $280K HECM = **~$1.02M** net real estate.
- Plus other assets (bank, brokerage, retirement, life insurance owned by John, personal property): unknown — must be inventoried.
- **If gross estate < $2M:** No MA estate tax. Likely outcome.
- **If > $2M:** Tax applies only to the excess.

Federal estate tax (~$13.99M+ exclusion in 2026) is irrelevant. MA has no gift tax, but the federal annual gift exclusion is $19K per donee in 2026. **Confirm 2026 federal exclusion with the family's CPA** — the 2025 reconciliation bill modified the TCJA sunset and the published number has shifted multiple times.

Source: [Mass.gov — Estate Tax Guide](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/massachusetts-estate-tax-guide) · [Mass.gov — FAQs: New Estate Tax Changes](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/faqs-new-estate-tax-changes) · [Nixon Peabody — MA Estate Tax Exemption $2M](https://www.nixonpeabody.com/insights/articles/2023/10/04/massachusetts-estate-tax-exemption-2000000-as-of-january-1-2023).

### G. MassHealth long-term care — the look-back and estate recovery

If long-term skilled-nursing care becomes necessary (median MA cost ~$160K/year), MassHealth becomes the only realistic payer for most middle-income families. Three rules dominate:

1. **60-month look-back (130 CMR 520.019).** Uncompensated transfers within 60 months of a long-term-care MassHealth application create transfer penalties.
2. **Trust treatment.** Assets in a *revocable* trust are **countable** for MassHealth (the grantor still has revocation power). A properly drafted *irrevocable* MAPT, after the 5-year clock has run, makes the principal non-countable. The MA SJC's *Daley v. Secretary of EOHHS*, 477 Mass. 188 (2017), confirmed that retaining a right of use and occupancy of a home conveyed to a MAPT does not make the home a countable asset.
3. **Estate recovery (130 CMR 515.011).** MassHealth recovers paid benefits from the estate of a deceased member who received long-term-care benefits at age 55+ or in a nursing facility. **Currently the recovery reaches the *probate* estate only.** A funded revocable trust passes outside probate at death and is therefore **outside MassHealth recovery** under the current regulation. *Verify against the most recent EOHHS bulletin before relying on this for planning — the 2003 attempted expansion to non-probate assets has been on-and-off.*

**Trade-off for an 85-year-old:** Actuarial life expectancy for an 85-year-old MA male is ~5.7 years (2024 SSA tables). The 5-year MAPT clock is a real consideration but not a slam-dunk decision. Common practitioner approach: revocable trust as primary plan, plus MAPT created and funded contemporaneously to start the clock with assets the father can afford to "lock up." The HECM home itself is hard to push into a MAPT without lender drama; usually it stays in the revocable trust with the MAPT used for liquid assets.

Source: [130 CMR 515.011 (LII)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/massachusetts/130-CMR-515-011) · [130 CMR 520.024 (Justia)](https://regulations.justia.com/states/massachusetts/130-cmr/title-130-cmr-520-000/section-520-024/) · [*Daley v. Secretary of EOHHS* (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/massachusetts/supreme-court/2017/sjc-12200.html) · [BBA Journal — Practical Guide to MassHealth Estate Recovery](https://bostonbar.org/journal/a-practical-guide-to-masshealth-estate-recovery-2/) · [Mass.gov — Estate Recovery FAQ (PDF)](https://www.mass.gov/doc/estate-recovery-frequently-asked-questions-0/download).

### H. Massachusetts Homestead (M.G.L. c. 188) — file the elderly declaration

- **§ 4 — Automatic homestead:** $125,000 of equity, no filing required.
- **§ 3 — Declared homestead:** $500,000 of equity, requires recording.
- **§ 2 — Elderly (62+) or disabled:** **$500,000 personal coverage**, file individually.

Homestead protects against most unsecured creditor claims; **does NOT protect against** the HECM lender, federal/state tax liens, child/spousal support, MassHealth recovery, or fraud-based debts. **Action:** if no Declaration is currently on file for Preston Beach Rd, file an **Elderly Declaration of Homestead under § 2** at the Essex South Registry of Deeds. Trivial cost (~$35), durable benefit. The homestead survives transfer into a revocable trust under § 5 if the trust ownership is recited in the declaration.

Source: [M.G.L. c. 188](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartII/TitleI/Chapter188/Section1) · [Mass Legal Help — Homestead Protection](https://www.masslegalhelp.org/money-debt/debt-collection/homestead-protection-law).

### I. Durable POA, healthcare proxy, HIPAA

**Durable POA (M.G.L. c. 190B, §§ 5-501 — 5-507).** MA has no statutory short form. The POA must contain durability language (e.g., "shall not be affected by subsequent disability or incapacity of the principal"); without it, the POA terminates the moment John becomes incapacitated. Notarization is not technically required by statute but is required as a practical matter — banks, brokerages, and the Registry will not accept un-notarized POAs. Standard practice: **two witnesses + notarization**. Naming **both children severally** (either child can act alone) is more practical for routine banking; **jointly** for major real-estate transactions. Document language must explicitly say so.

**Healthcare proxy (M.G.L. c. 201D).** Must be in writing and signed before two adult witnesses. The named agent cannot be a witness. Authority activates only when the attending physician determines (in writing) that the principal lacks capacity. Use the [Mass.gov standard form](https://www.mass.gov/doc/download-the-massachusetts-healthcare-proxy-form/download).

**HIPAA release (45 C.F.R. § 164.508).** Separate document; without it, healthcare-proxy agents may face provider stonewalling in pre-incapacity discussions. Standard practice is to execute a HIPAA release naming both children plus any other key family member.

**MOLST.** Optional physician-signed order set for an 85-year-old with serious illness; not required at this stage.

Source: [M.G.L. c. 190B § 5-501](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/mass-general-laws-c190b-ss-5-501) · [§ 5-502](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/mass-general-laws-c190b-ss-5-502) · [M.G.L. c. 201D](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartII/TitleII/Chapter201D/Section2).

## 9. Red Flags to Avoid

1. **Quitclaim deed of the home to the children.** Triggers (a) loss of step-up — children take $65K basis = ~$300K in avoidable cap-gains tax; (b) 60-month MassHealth transfer penalty; (c) **HECM default and acceleration** of the entire $280K (transferring title to a non-borrower is a maturity event under 24 C.F.R. § 206.27); (d) federal taxable gift requiring Form 709; (e) exposure of the home to the children's creditors and any future divorce.
2. **Adding the children as joint tenants on the deed.** All of the HECM, MassHealth, and gift-tax issues, plus only **partial step-up** at death and an immediate present gift.
3. **Deeding into a trust without HECM-servicer consent first.** Even an eligible trust must be vetted by the servicer; an unconsented transfer can be flagged as default. Always: draft → servicer review → written approval → record deed.
4. **Naming a single child as POA / sole successor trustee with two siblings.** Unequal authority is a leading source of family conflict and litigation. Better: co-trustees / co-agents acting **severally** for routine matters, **jointly** for major transactions; both children should see drafts.
5. **DIY trust documents (LegalZoom, online templates).** MUTC has specific drafting expectations (§ 813 notice, trustee acceptance, revocation methodology). Non-conforming trusts create title-insurance objections at the registry, require curative affidavits, and confuse HECM servicers.
6. **Failing to fund the trust.** Signed-but-unfunded = expensive paper. Deed must be recorded; accounts retitled; beneficiaries coordinated.
7. **Forgetting beneficiary designations.** Retirement accounts, life insurance, annuities pass by **beneficiary designation, not by will or trust**. Stale beneficiaries (deceased sibling, ex-spouse, "my estate") create probate exposure on those assets even if the home is perfectly trusted.
8. **Underestimating MassHealth estate-recovery exposure.** A will-only plan leaves the home in the probate estate at death — recoverable up to the full value of MassHealth benefits paid. The single most powerful estate-recovery defense for a moderate estate is a **funded revocable trust during life**.
9. **Filing homestead in the wrong name after retitling.** If the deed is changed to "[Father], Trustee," the homestead must be re-declared with the trust ownership recited — failure breaks protection.
10. **Reverse-mortgage default through neglect.** HECM also defaults if the borrower fails to (a) pay property taxes, (b) maintain hazard/flood insurance, or (c) maintain the property. **Preston Beach Rd is in or near coastal flood zones — verify flood insurance is current.** Under POA, the children should be able to step in to pay these obligations directly if John becomes unable.
